## 🎪 Conferences — Where Careers Are Built

### Global Tier 1 Conferences

**DEF CON** (Las Vegas, August)
The largest hacker conference in the world. Attendance: 30,000+. The **Villages** are free within the paid conference and often the most valuable part: AI Village, Blue Team Village, ICS Village, AppSec Village, Social Engineering Village, Career Village. The Career Village specifically has hiring managers and practitioners doing informal interviews. Talks are published on YouTube after the event.

**Black Hat USA** (Las Vegas, August — same week as DEF CON)
More enterprise/corporate than DEF CON. Expensive. Briefings require tickets; Business Hall is free. Best for: understanding where enterprise security money is going, networking with vendors, hearing cutting-edge research. Career-wise, DEF CON is more valuable per dollar.

**RSA Conference** (San Francisco, May)
The largest security business conference. Most relevant for: GRC professionals, security executives, vendors. Less relevant for technical practitioners early in their career. The free Expo floor has genuine value for meeting vendors and understanding the landscape.

### Regional Conferences — More Accessible, Often Better Networking

**BSides** (Global — 200+ cities)
BSides is a community-run conference network that happens in almost every major city globally. Tickets are low-cost or free. The talks are peer-reviewed practitioner content. **This is where most people should start.** Find your local BSides at securitybsides.com.

## 🧩 CTF (Capture The Flag) Communities

### What CTFs Do for Your Career
- Build skills that cannot be built from courses or labs alone
- CTFtime.org profile is a visible credential — hiring managers in offensive security check it
- Team membership demonstrates collaboration and community standing
- Writeups from CTF solutions are some of the best technical blog content available

### How to Start
1. **CTFtime.org**: The authoritative directory of CTF events worldwide. Filter by difficulty. Start with "beginner" rated events.
2. **picoCTF**: Carnegie Mellon's year-round beginner CTF. Best starting point for absolute beginners.
3. **CyberDefenders**: Blue team focused CTFs — DFIR, log analysis, malware analysis scenarios.
4. **National Cyber League (NCL)**: Twice-yearly competition, US-focused but open to all.

### Finding or Starting a CTF Team
- Post in r/netsec, HackTheBox forums, or your local BSides Slack that you are looking for a team
- Most CTF teams are happy to absorb committed beginners
- If none exist locally, start one — a 3–4 person team is enough to compete meaningfully

## 💼 LinkedIn Strategy — More Than Connection Requests

### The Wrong Way to Use LinkedIn
- Sending connection requests with no message to people you do not know
- Posting "I am looking for opportunities" as your only content
- Copying and pasting generic congratulations on other people's posts
- Using LinkedIn only as a job board

### The Right Way to Use LinkedIn

**Consume strategically**:
Follow 20–30 security practitioners who post regularly. Do not follow vendors or brand accounts — follow individuals. Your feed should teach you something every day.

Practitioners worth following (sample — not exhaustive): Dmitri Alperovitch, Bruce Schneier, Kelly Shortridge, Naomi Buckwalter, Troy Hunt, Graham Cluley, Chris Krebs.

**Create

**Create deliberately**:
Post 1–2 times per week. Good post types:
- "Here is what I learned building [project]" — most valuable post type
- Technical write-up of something you solved or built
- Honest reflection on a career challenge and what you learned
- Response/commentary on industry news (with your actual opinion, not just "great article!")

**Engage specifically**:
When you comment on someone's post, say something specific and substantive — not "great point!" If you disagree, say so respectfully. This is how practitioners become known.

**Reach out with context**:
When connecting with someone you don't know: one sentence on who you are and one sentence on why you want to connect. Specific > generic every time.
