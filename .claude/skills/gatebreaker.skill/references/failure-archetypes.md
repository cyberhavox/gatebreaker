## 📚 REFERENCE LIBRARY: FAILURE-ARCHETYPES

# The Six Career Failure Archetypes — Pattern Recognition & Recovery

> *"Most people are not failing because they are incapable. They are failing because they are trapped in a pattern they cannot see from inside it."*

These are the six most common failure patterns in cybersecurity careers. Each archetype has a **diagnosis checklist**, the **psychology** driving it, a **hard-truth intervention**, and a **concrete recovery plan**. Read carefully. Most people fit more than one.

---

## 🏅 ARCHETYPE 1: The Cert Collector

### Profile
Has 4–7 certifications. Possibly Security+, CEH, CySA+, CompTIA Linux+, maybe a CISSP attempt. Zero home lab. Zero GitHub. Zero CTF history. Zero deployed projects. Cannot get past an HR screen or a technical phone call.

### Diagnosis Checklist
- [ ] You have more than 3 certs but fewer than 3 hands-on projects you can demo
- [ ] Your resume lists certifications before experience
- [ ] You feel productive while studying for certs but anxious when asked to "just do the thing"
- [ ] You have failed a technical interview that asked you to do something instead of explain it
- [ ] You believe one more cert will fix the application problem

### The Psychology
Certifications feel like progress because they have a clear endpoint — a pass/fail result that delivers a dopamine hit. Hands-on work has no such clear endpoint. It is ambiguous, uncomfortable, and exposes gaps immediately. The cert becomes a substitute for the harder thing. Each failed job application gets rationalized as "I just need one more credential."

### Hard Truth
**No hiring manager has ever looked at a candidate's fifth certification and thought "this is the one that makes them hireable."** Certifications prove you can pass a test. Projects prove you can do the work. After Security+, every additional cert without a matching project returns diminishing value. You are not more qualified than you were two certs ago. You are more credentialed and equally unqualified.

### Recovery Plan
1. **Stop buying courses for 90 days.** Full stop. No exceptions.
2. **Build one project this month** from `references/portfolio-projects.md` — pick the domain you want to work in and start the Tier 1 project
3. **Document it publicly** — GitHub repo with a README that explains what you built, what you learned, what went wrong
4. **Write one LinkedIn post** about what you built — not "I passed my exam," but "here is what I built and what I learned"
5. After 90 days, evaluate whether applications are getting further than before. They will be.

## 🎧 ARCHETYPE 2: The Passive Learner

### Profile
Has completed 300–800 hours of video courses across Udemy, Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, YouTube. Knows what tools are called. Cannot use them. Opens a terminal and freezes. Would describe their skill level as "intermediate" but fails every hands-on lab.

### Diagnosis Checklist
- [ ] You can explain what Wireshark does but have never captured and analyzed real traffic
- [ ] You know what a SQL injection is but have never successfully executed one in a lab
- [ ] You use the phrase "I've watched a course on that" as an answer to "can you do this?"
- [ ] Your notes are beautifully organized. Your terminal history is empty.
- [ ] You feel like you know a lot but get anxious when someone asks you to prove it

### The Psychology
Passive consumption feels like learning. The brain registers new information as progress. But knowledge without application decays within weeks — studies show retention of passive learning drops to under 10% without practice. The comfort of watching is preferable to the discomfort of failing in a lab. The courses keep coming because each new course promises to finally "click."

### Hard Truth
**You do not know what you think you know.** You know what the instructor knows. Until you have failed at something in a terminal, debugged it, and made it work, you do not know it. Every hour of passive video you watch beyond the first pass is a choice to avoid real learning. TryHackMe exists. HackTheBox exists. Immersive Labs exists. The lab is where learning actually happens.

### Recovery Plan
1. **Close the video tabs.** You have enough background knowledge — start applying it.
2. **Create a TryHackMe or HackTheBox account today** — complete one room/box per week minimum
3. **The rule**: for every 30 minutes of video you watch, spend 60 minutes in a lab on the same topic
4. **Document failures** — every time something doesn't work, write down why and what you did to fix it. This becomes your portfolio.
5. **Join a CTF** within the next 30 days. Finishing last is fine. Finishing is not the goal — doing is.

## 📚 ARCHETYPE 3: The Perpetual Student

### Profile
Has been "preparing to apply" for 18–36 months. Always one course away, one cert away, one project away. Has a detailed study plan. Has rewritten the study plan multiple times. Has never submitted more than 10 job applications. May have an impressive knowledge base but paralyzing self-doubt about readiness.

### Diagnosis Checklist
- [ ] You have delayed applying because you "aren't ready yet" for more than 6 months
- [ ] You have researched job requirements extensively but concluded you still need more preparation
- [ ] When someone asks why you haven't applied, your answer starts with "once I finish..."
- [ ] You have changed your target role or domain more than twice in the last year
- [ ] You feel like you need to be 100% qualified before applying, even for entry-level roles

### The Psychology
This is usually fear of rejection — specifically, the fear that rejection will confirm that you are genuinely not good enough. As long as you have not applied, the possibility that you would succeed remains open. Perpetual preparation is a rational strategy for protecting your self-concept from a verdict you are afraid of hearing. The study plan becomes a shield.

### Hard Truth
**You will never feel ready. That feeling does not go away with more preparation — it gets worse, because the standard you set for yourself keeps rising.** Entry-level roles do not require mastery. They require demonstrable effort, intellectual curiosity, and the willingness to learn. You are 80% more ready than you think you are for the roles you are avoiding. The market will tell you where your gaps are faster and more accurately than any study plan. Apply now. Fix gaps based on actual feedback.

### Recovery Plan
1. **Set a hard deadline**: "I will have submitted 20 applications by [date 30 days from now]." Write it down.
2. **Lower the bar for "ready"** — if you meet 60% of a job description's requirements, apply
3. **Track rejections as data** — every rejection tells you something real about the gap. Celebrate the data.
4. **Apply for one "reach" role per week** — you will not get it, but the interview practice is irreplaceable
5. **Find an accountability partner** — someone who will ask you every week: "How many applications did you submit?"

## 🗺️ ARCHETYPE 4: The Wrong Track

### Profile
Has been preparing seriously for a role that does not actually match their goals, skills, or personality. Common examples: pursuing CISSP/GRC for years while secretly wanting to do red team. Grinding SOC work while wanting to do cloud security. Taking management roles to earn more money but hating every day of people management.

### Diagnosis Checklist
- [ ] You dread the work you are preparing for or doing, even though you "should" want it
- [ ] You chose your track based on salary data, job availability, or someone else's suggestion — not genuine interest
- [ ] You consume content from a different domain "just for fun" and feel energized in a way your actual track doesn't give you
- [ ] You justify staying on your current track because of sunk cost ("I've already spent 2 years on this")
- [ ] When you imagine doing your target role for 10 years, you feel dread not excitement

### The Psychology
The wrong-track pattern is often driven by external validation — a well-meaning family member, a salary comparison article, a job market trend. People also stay on the wrong track because of sunk cost fallacy: the belief that time already invested makes changing direction more costly than continuing. In reality, a year on the right track vastly outperforms three more years on the wrong one.

### Hard Truth
**There is no version of this where grinding a career you dislike leads to excellent outcomes.** You will underperform relative to people who actually want to do the work. You will burn out faster. You will learn slower. The "practical" choice that makes you miserable will make you less competitive, not more. The sunk cost is gone. The future time is not. Cut your losses now.

### Recovery Plan
1. **Name the track you actually want** — be honest with yourself for one hour, with no practical constraints
2. **Read `references/career-phases.md`** — find the phase/domain that genuinely excites you
3. **Do a 30-day pilot**: spend 30 days genuinely exploring the domain you actually want — labs, reading, community
4. **Audit the transition cost**: what certs/skills transfer from your current track? (Often more than you think)
5. **Set a transition timeline** — not "someday" but a specific date by which you have made the move or decided to commit

## 👻 ARCHETYPE 5: The Overqualified Ghost

### Profile
5–15 years of IT or adjacent experience. Transitioning into cybersecurity or trying to move into a more senior security role. Consistently undersells themselves. Uses language like "I'm just a sysadmin" or "I don't have formal security experience." Applies for roles two tiers below their actual capability. Gets ignored or rejected because their resume reads as a different person than they are in conversation.

### Diagnosis Checklist
- [ ] Your resume does not mention security work you have actually done (patching, firewall rules, incident response, hardening)
- [ ] You describe your background with apologetic language ("just," "only," "basic," "some experience with")
- [ ] You have been in IT for 5+ years and are applying for entry-level security roles
- [ ] Your LinkedIn profile reads like a junior candidate but your conversation reads like a senior one
- [ ] Hiring managers who meet you in person seem surprised by how much you know

### The Psychology
Impostor syndrome is the primary driver here, amplified by the transition context. "Real" cybersecurity experience feels like a different category from the security-adjacent work that IT professionals do every day. The result is a candidate who systematically devalues everything relevant they have done and positions themselves as a beginner when they are not.

### Hard Truth
**You are not a beginner. Stop presenting yourself as one.** Every sysadmin who has hardened a server, managed a firewall, responded to a malware incident, or written a security policy has done cybersecurity work. The difference between IT and security is increasingly semantic. Your experience is an asset that most genuine beginners cannot compete with. Using it as an apology is leaving money, opportunities, and career years on the table.

### Recovery Plan
1. **Audit your real experience** — list every security-relevant thing you have done in your IT career, no matter how small
2. **Rewrite your resume** starting from `references/resume-linkedin.md` — frame every relevant IT task as security work (because it was)
3. **Stop applying for Tier 1 roles** — you belong at Tier 2 or Tier 3. Apply there.
4. **Practice saying** "I have X years of hands-on experience with [specific security-relevant work]" out loud until it does not feel like lying
5. **Get one external validation** — a mentor, a recruiter, or a technical peer who will honestly tell you your real market tier

## 🏆 ARCHETYPE 6: The Title Chaser

### Profile
Optimizes career decisions for job title, salary, and status rather than skill development. Moves into management before they are technically deep enough. Takes the CISO title at a small company with no security program and no support, then cannot do the job. Gets promoted into leadership because they communicate well, then struggles when technical depth is required.

### Diagnosis Checklist
- [ ] You have accepted a management or leadership role primarily for the title or salary bump
- [ ] You struggle to do the hands-on work your reports do — and avoid situations where this would be exposed
- [ ] You have not written code, run a scan, or touched a CLI in your current role in more than 12 months
- [ ] You compensate for technical gaps with strong process/communication skills — and feel anxious when that is not enough
- [ ] Your career decisions have been driven more by "what looks good" than "what will make me better"

### The Psychology
Career progress is socially visible through title and compensation. The pressure — especially in high-achieving environments — to demonstrate upward trajectory creates incentives to optimize for the visible markers of success rather than the underlying capability. The result is a title that outpaces the skill, creating a fragile position: difficult to defend in a downturn, difficult to perform in without support, and difficult to translate to a new employer.

### Hard Truth
**A CISO title from a 50-person company with no security team, no budget, and no wins does not make you a CISO.** It makes you someone who held a title. The market can tell the difference. Technical depth is not optional for security leaders — it is what separates leaders who can be respected by their teams from those who merely manage them. If you have been chasing titles over skills, you now have a gap that will catch up with you, usually at the worst possible moment.

### Recovery Plan
1. **Honestly assess your technical floor** — what can you actually do, right now, with your hands?
2. **Pick one technical domain to go deep in** over the next 6 months — not broad, deep
3. **Do one hands-on project** per quarter that proves technical capability — document it
4. **Stop accepting titles you cannot back up** — a lateral move to a smaller title at a better company with real technical challenge is worth more long-term
5. **Find a technical mentor who will be honest with you** about the gap and hold you to closing it
