# First 90 Days — Onboarding Plans for Every Career Tier

> *"Getting the job is the beginning, not the achievement. What you do in the first 90 days determines whether you keep it, grow in it, or become a cautionary tale."*

The first 90 days of any new security role are the highest-leverage period of your entire tenure. You have permission to ask questions, observe, and learn. You have no legacy decisions to defend. You have political capital you have not yet spent. This window closes fast.

---

## 🧭 Universal Principles for Every Tier

**Day 1–3: Listen before you speak.**
Do not arrive with solutions. You do not yet understand the problem. Every organization has context — history, politics, technical debt, relationships — that is invisible from the outside. The new person who arrives with opinions on Day 1 is always wrong, even when they are technically right.

**Day 1–30: Map before you change.**
Understand what exists before deciding what should be different. Every tool, process, and policy that seems wrong was probably the right decision at some point. Know the why before you propose the what.

**Day 31–60: Contribute quietly.**
Your first visible contributions should be small, reliable, and delivered on time. One small thing done well builds more trust than three big things attempted and partially completed.

**Day 61–90: Own one thing.**
By day 90, you should own one specific, scoped responsibility — not a massive project, but something real that the team can point to as yours. This establishes your professional identity in the organization.

## 🟢 TIER 1–2: Entry-Level Analyst / Junior Practitioner

*Applies to: SOC Analyst L1/L2, Junior Penetration Tester, Junior AppSec Engineer, IT Security Analyst*

### Days 1–30: Learn the Landscape

**Technical priorities**:
- [ ] Learn every tool in the stack — SIEM, EDR, ticketing system, communication platforms — at a basic operational level
- [ ] Shadow senior analysts for the first 2 weeks; do not jump to independent work before you understand the workflow
- [ ] Read the last 6 months of incident tickets/reports — understand what "normal" looks like here
- [ ] Map the network/architecture (even a rough sketch). Do not assume you understand the environment until you can draw it.
- [ ] Identify the 3–5 alert types you will handle most frequently and understand the investigation process for each

**People priorities**:
- [ ] Learn every team member's name, role, and area of expertise in week 1
- [ ] Identify your go-to person for each tool/system — not always your direct manager
- [ ] Ask questions early and often — but batch them (don't interrupt every 10 minutes)
- [ ] Find out how the team likes to communicate: Slack vs. email vs. in-person

**What NOT to do**:
- Do not suggest that the current process is wrong yet — you don't know enough
- Do not skip documentation because you plan to do it later
- Do not try to impress anyone — try to be reliable

### Days 31–60: Start Contributing

**Technical priorities**:
- [ ] Handle a defined category of alerts or tasks independently with minimal hand-holding
- [ ] Write up one incident report, walkthrough, or runbook improvement without being asked
- [ ] Ask your manager what metric or output matters most to them — align your work to it
- [ ] Find one thing in the environment that is documented poorly and volunteer to improve it

**People priorities**:
- [ ] Attend team meetings actively — not silently
- [ ] Share one useful thing per week (article, technique, tool tip) with the team — start building your knowledge-sharing identity
- [ ] Ask for informal feedback: "Is there anything I should be doing differently?"

### Days 61–90: Own Something

**Technical priorities**:
- [ ] Own a specific alert category, detection rule maintenance, or recurring report
- [ ] Complete one project that was on the backlog — even a small one
- [ ] Build or document a runbook for a process that does not currently have one

**People priorities**:
- [ ] Request a formal 90-day check-in with your manager
- [ ] Come to that meeting with: what you have learned, what you have contributed, what you want to develop next
- [ ] Identify your 6-month goal — share it with your manager and ask for their input

## 🔵 TIER 3–4: Mid-Level / Senior Practitioner

*Applies to: Senior SOC Analyst, Penetration Tester, Threat Hunter, Cloud Security Engineer, AppSec Engineer, Security Engineer*

### Days 1–30: Understand the Technical and Political Reality

**Technical priorities**:
- [ ] Map the full security toolset: what exists, what is used well, what is shelfware, what is missing
- [ ] Review the last 3 major incidents or findings — what was the response? What would you have done differently?
- [ ] Identify the top 3 security gaps from your vantage point — but keep this list to yourself for now
- [ ] Understand the detection/prevention coverage model: what is monitored, what is not, and why
- [ ] Meet the vendors/tools you inherit — understand licensing, renewal timelines, support relationships

**People priorities**:
- [ ] Meet every stakeholder who intersects with your work: IT ops, dev teams, compliance, leadership
- [ ] Identify the informal power structure — who actually makes decisions vs. who has the title
- [ ] Find the most respected senior practitioner on the team and cultivate that relationship intentionally

**What NOT to do**:
- Do not implement significant changes in the first 30 days — even if you can see the right answer
- Do not publicly criticize decisions made before you arrived
- Do not assume the previous approach was incompetence — it may have been the right call with the constraints that existed

### Days 31–60: Validate Your Assessment and Start Contributing

**Technical priorities**:
- [ ] Validate your gap analysis with a second perspective — a trusted colleague or your manager
- [ ] Execute one scoped improvement that delivers visible value (new detection, improved runbook, reduced false positive rate)
- [ ] Present a brief "state of [your domain]" summary to your manager — shows situational awareness and analytical thinking

**People priorities**:
- [ ] Identify the one stakeholder relationship that most needs improvement and invest in it
- [ ] Start being visible in cross-team conversations — not loudly, but consistently present

### Days 61–90: Establish Your Domain and Propose a 6-Month Plan

**Technical priorities**:
- [ ] Have a clear ownership area: what you are responsible for, what success looks like in 6 months
- [ ] Draft a 6-month roadmap for your domain — present it to your manager before Day 90
- [ ] Deliver one significant output: a hardened environment component, a completed assessment, a working detection suite, etc.

**People priorities**:
- [ ] Request a 90-day review. Bring data — what you contributed, what you learned, what you plan to do next
- [ ] Identify one junior person to mentor or support — this is how senior practitioners build reputation

## 🔴 TIER 5–6: Security Manager / Director / CISO

*Applies to: Security Manager, Director of Security, VP of Security, CISO*

### Days 1–30: Listen, Assess, Do Not Decide

**Strategic priorities**:
- [ ] Meet every direct report 1:1 in the first two weeks — listen more than you speak
- [ ] Meet every key business stakeholder: CEO/COO, CTO, CIO, Legal/Compliance, Finance, major business unit heads
- [ ] Understand the security program's history: what was built, what was tried and failed, what the previous leader's priorities were
- [ ] Do a rapid risk assessment: what are the top 3 risks the organization faces right now?
- [ ] Understand the budget reality: current FY spend, headcount, contracts, renewal schedule

**People priorities**:
- [ ] Assess your team honestly — who is strong, who is struggling, who is in the wrong role
- [ ] Do not make personnel decisions in the first 30 days — you do not yet have enough information
- [ ] Identify the 2–3 people on your team who are the institutional memory — invest in those relationships

**What NOT to do**:
- Do not announce a strategy in your first month — you do not know enough yet
- Do not make major vendor decisions or sign large contracts in the first 60 days
- Do not reorganize the team in the first 90 days unless there is an active crisis

### Days 31–60: Validate, Build Trust, Communicate a Vision

**Strategic priorities**:
- [ ] Share your preliminary risk assessment with your CEO/board sponsor — frame it as a conversation, not a presentation
- [ ] Identify 1–2 quick wins: visible improvements that demonstrate competence without requiring major investment
- [ ] Build your external network within the first 60 days: peer CISOs, your legal counsel, your key vendor contacts
- [ ] Understand the regulatory and compliance obligations the organization carries

**People priorities**:
- [ ] Communicate your management style explicitly to your team: how you like to be updated, how you make decisions, what you care about
- [ ] Begin informal succession planning thinking: who on your team could step up?

### Days 61–90: Propose the Strategy and Own the Risk Narrative

**Strategic priorities**:
- [ ] Deliver a security program assessment and 12-month strategic plan to your executive sponsor and board (if applicable)
- [ ] Establish your risk reporting cadence: how often, in what format, to which audiences
- [ ] Close at least one significant gap that was identified in your first 30 days
- [ ] Establish your external peer relationships — you will need them for threat intel, industry context, and career resilience

**Leadership priorities**:
- [ ] Have a clear answer to: "What does success look like for you in your first year?"
- [ ] Ensure every direct report knows what is expected of them and how they will be evaluated
- [ ] Begin building your board/executive communication style — learn what they care about and speak to it

## 🔁 The 90-Day Retrospective — Ask Yourself

After Day 90, every new hire at every tier should reflect:

1. **What did I learn that surprised me?** (What was different from what I expected?)
2. **What did I contribute that the team actually valued?**
3. **What relationship do I most need to invest in going forward?**
4. **What is the one thing I wish I had done differently in the first 90 days?**
5. **What is my #1 priority for the next 90 days?**

If you cannot answer these five questions clearly, you are not yet oriented in your role — and that needs to change before Month 4.
